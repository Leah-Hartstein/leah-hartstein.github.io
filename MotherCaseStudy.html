Overview

Fast fashion is really crazy. It's come to dominate how we buy all our clothes but its really bad for the environment and the people who have to actually make the damn garments. According to a study by whoever it led to lots and lots of carbon emissions and other nasty pollutant stuff.

I tackled this issue as part of a group of four for a course called 'Designing for Social Impact' in the first semester of my third year of university, where we were challenged to create a socially-conscious app addressing one of the 16 United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), we chose goal 16, Responsible Production and Consumption.

We considered several options for creating alternatives to fast fashion before landing on a solution that's novel and a lot of fun: changing the purchasing habits of consumers with an app that challenges them to justify their fast fashion purchases at the point of sale.

Though we were working in a very limited timeframe, and only had time to explore solutions on mobile platforms, I'm extremely proud of what we were able to make.

Research

After first forming a group, we spent our first tutorial reading over the full list of 16 UN SDGs and considering issues that we would be able to address within the scope of our eventual final deliverable of a UI prototype. After extensive discussion, we all landed on the idea of exploring fast fashion through UN SDG 12, as we all had a mutual interest in fashion and all shared the experience of making too many impulse purchases. We then split up responsibilities for our research. I volunteered to do most of the secondary research, since I'm pretty skilled in it from my previous degree. Primary research was split between my other three group members, with each choosing to do interviews, surveys and online ethnography, respectively.

We found a remarkable thread of insights across all of our research methods. I was somewhat aware of the issues with fast fashion, but I still found my secondary research into reports from NGOs and other fashion industry researchers to be quite shocking. Essentially: Fast fashion is horrifically bad for the environment, responsible for [number] of carbon waste annually and [percentage] of carbon emissions. Production is spread across the entire world, with it being extremely common for a garment to pass through several different countries in its production process before final retail sale to first-world consumers. Surveys of consumers find this to be pretty common knowledge for the general public and polls show widespread knowledge of the flaws of the production process. However, this awareness does not greatly influence consumer purchases because consumers operate under very limited knowledge and do not trust more expensive 'sustainably-made' brands to be significantly better.

After collating the primary research conducted by my groupmembers with an affinity diagram, we found it correlated very strongly with the insights from my secondary research. [something from interviews and stuff idk]. We knew from this that addressing fast fashion by selling more sustainable clothes is not enough of a solution, and some sort of intervention to just simply make consumers by less clothes is necessary.

We synthesised all of our findings into an affinity diagram (which can be seen here.), and a single problem statement to orient our design moving forward:

While most consumers are aware of the social, environmental, and ethical challenges posed by fast fashion, their capacity to adopt more sustainable practices is constrained by time and budget.

Ideation

With a strong idea for the shape of our potential solution, each group member was tasked with coming up with an original idea for a potential solution, targeted at people like us: fashion-conscious consumers under the age of 30.

We landed on three proposed solutions:

  • 'Sewlution', an app to suggest repairs and alterations for already-owned clothes.
  • 'The People's Wardobe', a system of communal wardrobe rental.
  • My idea, 'SSHAME', an app and browser extension that confronts the user at the point of sale and challenges them to list times they will actually wear the garment.

We then compared our three ideas with a decision matrix, ranking each on how they address core user needs, the winner was SSHAME, as we concluded that it was the most novel, most focused on causing positive behavioural shifts, and the easiest to show with a figma prototype.

Final Concepts

Prototyping

With a final idea we moved forward to our first wireframes and prototypes. I did sketches to flesh out my initial idea into a basic UI flow, in the form of an app overlay that slides over other fashion apps when a purchase is about to be made. To incentivise further engagement, the user can also catalogue their wardobe in the app, and see purchases made by friends.

My groupmembers then got to work on the first low-fidelity wireframes. Our goal was to establish the full scope of the app, so the focus was mostly on the design and page layout, and a set of core features. Initially, this included more ambitious ideas for 3D user avatar that clothing could be 'previewed' on, a wishlist, and a full social network style interface for sharing outfits with friends. Though its good to encourage users to think more about their purchaes, we felt that these ideas were too ambitious and too much of a distraction for the user to navigate, so they were cut from the final prototype.

It's here where we also started to decide on a brand identity for the app. SSHAME was a funny idea for the initial pitch and was well recieved, but we felt that a system of entirely negative reinforcement wasn't ideal for changing user habits in the long term. I came up with a new name: Mother, evoking the earth, queer slang for dressing well, and gentle dissaproval from an authority figure.

Final Prototype

Our final high-fidelity prototype refined our concept down to a simple core loop with a strong aesthetic. Instead of a more elaborate prolonged experience, we shifted to a simple lightweight app for logging purchases, and not-purchases, then tracking and cataloguing your progress over time akin to apps like Letterboxd.

We conducted three rounds of think-aloud testing and found that the premise of the app was well-understould and enjoyed by our users, with only minor suggestions for changes to things like icons and colours to ensure that the interface of the 'overlay' portion of the app contrasts adequately with the fashion apps it 'interferes' with. It recieved a SUS (System Usability Scale) score of 97.

I designed the logo, choosing a clear black and white design of a sexy, Bratz-doll esque globe, conveying both the humour and fashion focus of the app.



Conclusion

Our final submission was very well-received by our peers and faculty and recieved a high distinction grade. After our presentation, a few people in the audience told me that they would use the app if it exists.

Though Mother is not a complete solution to the issue of fast fashion, something like it could be a good first step, and I'm very proud of the work we were able to do.
